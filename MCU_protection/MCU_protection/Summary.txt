This circuit protects a 3.3V microcontroller ADC input from fault conditions using a series current-limiting resistor and Schottky clamp diodes. During normal operation (e.g., a safe 1.5V analog signal), the diodes remain reverse-biased. The 1kΩ series resistor passes the signal to the Vprotected node with a minimal voltage drop across the 100kΩ ADC load, keeping the protected voltage well within ±5% of the input signal. During a +20V overvoltage fault, the upper diode forward-biases, safely shunting current to the 3.3V rail and clamping the output to a safe +3.6V. Conversely, during a -20V reverse-polarity fault, the lower diode conducts, clamping the pin to roughly -0.3V.

Two major changes were made from the reference design. First, the back-to-back 12V zeners were replaced with Schottky steering diodes clamped to a 3.3V rail and ground. This provides a sharper cutoff and prevents the "soft knee" leakage typical of low-voltage zeners from distorting valid signals. Second, the series resistor was increased from 10Ω to 1kΩ to safely limit the worst-case fault current to 19.7mA (during the -20V fault), protecting the clamp devices.

This design is highly useful for microcontroller analog input stages because it prevents destructive transients and wiring errors from damaging the sensitive, high-impedance silicon of the MCU's internal ADC.
